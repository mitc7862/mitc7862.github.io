The Hum

Imagine. It’s 1:00 AM and you can’t fall asleep. It's been weeks since you’ve had a full-night’s rest. Whenever you go to lay down the hum is there. Your spouse can't hear it. No-one seems to hear it but you. If you try, you can hear it during the day, but it’s especially loud at night after the world quiets down.

You've looked everywhere for the source—investigated every appliance, every socket and circuit in your house. You've even driven around the neighborhood late at night trying to pinpoint any possible origin.

Perhaps it's coming from inside you. You begin to supect some kind of hearing disorder. In the end all the medical tests, second and third opinions from doctors have yielded no resolution. You ask yourself, could it all be in my head? Will it ever stop?

The Hum is a name often given to widespread reports of a persistent and invasive humming, rumbling, or droning noise that only 2–4% of people can hear.

Thousands of people from all over the world have reported hearing the hum; the phenomenon has been widely reported by media in the United States and the UK. Sometimes it gets named according to the locality where the problem has been particularly publicized, such as the Taos Hum and the Windsor Hum.

In 2018, independent researcher, Glen McPherson began the World Hum Database, collecting information from those who hear the hum, hoping to one day solve the mystery so we can all get back to sleep. What follows is a look at the data he’s collected so far.

The hum heard around the world

…but mostly in Europe and North America

Where the hum is loudest

The majority of people in the World Hum Database reported hearing the hum loudest indoors. It’s hypothesized that certain low-frequency sounds could resonate with different types of construction and materials.

When the hum is loudest

Similarly, most people report hearing the hum louder at night or when they’re trying to fall asleep when there’s less ambient sound to mask lower frequencies.

What does the hum sound like?

“A wum-wum sound, like a very slowly idling large engine. Fades in and out.”

Infra-sound

7% of those who reported hearing the hum claimed to hear frequencies below the human range of hearing (20 Hz). These frequencies are known as infra-sound, and in some cases are thought to interact with human physiology in a way that the brain detects as sound—even though the human ear can’t hear them.

Tinnitus

Sometimes the hum is dismissed as a form of tinnitus, a ringing in the ears caused by various underlying conditions. However, most people who hear the hum report sounds far below the frequency range of tinnitus (5–10 kHz).
