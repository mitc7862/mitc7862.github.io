Almost 65% of Africa is covered by grasslands, including savannas.

The savanna biome is a tropical grassland with warm temperatures year-round and seasonal rainfall in the summer. It is characterized by grasses and dispersed trees that do not form a closed canopy, allowing sunlight to reach the ground. The African savanna contains a diverse community of organisms that interact to form a complex food web.

Ostrich

These flightless birds are the largest birds in the world. They feed on grasses, shrubbery, berries, seeds, and succulents. They also eat insects and small reptiles — which they chase in an awkward zigzag pattern.

Sickle bush

Sickle bush is a flowering shrub know for producing fruit an seeds. Many herbivores eat the plaint's juicy pods which fall on the ground. Such animals also feed on the shrub's immature twigs, and leaves.

Lanner falcon

Lanner falcons are medium-sized birds of prey. They mostly feed on smaller birds, but also feed on lizards, rodents, and bats. Lanner falcons require large open or lightly wooded hunting areas, as well as rocky formations such as cliffs for nesting.

Bacteria

Bacteria are key decomposers of any biome, their large numbers allow them to widely colonize a habitat's soil. Bacteria often flourish in savannas where temperatures tend to remain above 25 degrees Celsius.

Roan antelope

Roan antelope associate in herds of up to 35 and eat mostly grass. They must drink regularly and inhabit areas where water is easily accessible. They can be identified by their a black-and-white clown-like facemask, which is darker in males than females.

Jackal

Jackals are best described as opportunistic omnivores. They hunt small antelopes in packs and also eat reptiles, insects, ground-dwelling birds, fruits, berries, and grass. They'll even pick over kills made by large carnivores.

African hare

Hares spend most of the day lying in a “form”—a depression in the ground or under bushes. They do not dig burrows like rabbits do. Instead, they use dens or burrows already made by warthogs or aardvark. Hares eat leaves, buds, roots, fungi, bark, and twigs.

Aardvark

Aardvarks are solitary mammals, leaving their burrows at night to forage for termites or mate. They belong to the same group of mammals as elephants and have no relation to anteaters despite their similar appearance.

Springhare

These unusual hares looks like a cross between a kangaroo and a rabbit. They have large back legs that help them to make gigantic leaps, using their long tail for balance. Their much smaller forelimbs have very sharp claws, which they use to dig burrows.

Termite

Like ants, termites live in large hierarchical colonies consisting of workers, soldiers and reproductives, including the king and queen. They build large mounds and help aerate the soil allowing plants to flourish. The feed on decaying plant matter.

Lovebird

These brightly-colored small parrots eat seeds and grains. They have to drink often, so they tend to live near water. The lovebird is named for the strong bonds formed between mating pairs. These birds mate for life and are monogamous.

African Buffalo

Savanna buffaloes are heavy cow-like animals with large curved horns. They live in herds of a few hundred to protect from predators, but have been known to congregate in the thousands during the rainy season.

Grass

There are many types of grasses in the savanna, such as Rhodes grass, red oats grass and lemon grass. The Bermuda grass used in landscaping is native to the African savanna as is elephant grass, which can grow 3 meters tall.

Giraffe

Giraffes are the world’s tallest mammals. They are uniquely adapted to reach vegetation inaccessible to other herbivores. They feed sixteen to twenty hours a day. Acacia trees have been found to be their favorite food source.

Hyena

These carnivores are one of Africa’s top predators. However, they aren't primarily scavengers. About 70 percent of their diet is composed of direct kills. They consume animals of various types and sizes, carrion, bones, and other animal droppings.

Acacia tree

The acacia tree uses its long tap roots draw water from deep below the surface during the dry season. Its long thorns are home to small stinging ants, both of which ward off many herbivores. Only the giraffe can tolerate the acacia foliage.

Elephant

Elephants are the world’s largest land mammals. Their long trunks are used for breathing, smelling, drinking, trumpeting, and grabbing objects. They spend the majority of their day roaming across large distances to forage for grasses, roots, and bark.

Dung beetle

Once dung beetles find a supply of dung, they roll it into balls and move it away to avoid competition with other dung beetles. They bury their balls of dung and lay eggs in them, providing a stable food supply for their growing larva.

Impala

Able to both graze and browse, the impala has a greater and more reliable food supply than animals that do either one or the other. It eats young grass shoots in the rainy season and herbs and shrubs at other times.

Zebra

Zebras look like horses but with a mane of short straight hair and iconic black and white stripes. Every year, hundreds of thousands of zebras migrate 2,900 kilometers from Tanzania’s Serengeti plains, north to Kenya to find food and water.

Mongoose

Mongooses eat small rodents, reptiles, young birds, eggs, termites, grasshoppers, beetles, and spiders. They live in female-dominated groups of 12 to 15 and spend most of the day looking for food among the brush, leaves, and rocks.

Wildebeest

Wildebeest are large antelopes with box-like heads and curving horns. They migrate long distances seeking grass and water. By the end of the dry season, the Serengeti wildebeest have exhausted the grazing lands, returning home as the rains begin.

Warthog

Warthogs are tough, sturdy animals with large heads and “warts”—thick protective pads on both sides of their heads. They kneel on their calloused, padded knees to eat short grass and use their snouts and tusks to dig for bulbs, tubers, and roots.

Grasshopper

Grasshoppers are plant-eating insects. They eat some plants, allowing others to flourish, helping diversity stay in balance. Grasshoppers are a good food source for small predators.

Leopard

These powerful carnivores are covered in dark, irregular spots called rosettes. These spots are circular in east African leopards, but square in southern African leopards. They spend much of their time in trees even when stalking prey or eating.

Lion

Lions commonly eat antelope, zebra, and wildebeests but scavenged food provides more than 50 percent of their diets. Cooperative hunting enables them to take down prey as large as buffaloes, rhinos, hippos, and giraffes.

Ant

Africa contains many ant species. Ants live large colonies containing a queen, workers (female offspring of the queen), and soldiers (male offspring). Most ants are herbivores, but some—like the Driver ant—feed on other insects and small animals.

Guinea fowl

Guineafowl form flocks of up to 25 birds. Their diet consists of a variety of animal and plant food; seeds, fruits, greens, snails, spiders, worms and insects, lizards, snakes and small mammals.

White-backed
vulture

Unique adaptations make these birds one of the most successful scavengers in Africa. Strong eyesight enables them to detect potential meals from high above and Low acidity in their stomachs help digest rotting meat.

Baboon

Baboons are some of the world’s largest monkeys. They are omnivorous and will eat just about anything. Grass makes up a large part of their diet, along with food from a variety of plants. They also eat insects and small quantities of meat.

Striped skink

The striped skink is a medium sized lizard, characterized by a pale stripe running down each of its sides. They also have a transparent window in each lower eyelid and lobed ears. Their diet consists of small insects such as moths, beetles and termites.

Fungi

Because the African savanna has a very arid climate, there is less fungal growth than in damper areas. But, there are still some fungi that grow in the savanna such as turkey tail mushrooms, crimson cup fungi, and sulfur shelf fungi.

Maintaining balance in the savanna.

A community is a group of organisms interacting in the same environment. A food web shows how these organisms are linked in order of the food they eat, from producers to consumers, prey to predators, and scavengers to decomposers. The arrows in a food web represent the flow of energy and matter between organisms. In most ecosystems, organisms can get food and energy from many sources, and may have many predators.
